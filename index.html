Here you can explore the Mandelbrot and Julia fractals, see the equations behind the scenes, and the calculations used to render them. A fun little project devised to explore how the current web can handle data visualization and some heafty number crunching. Formatting equations TeX style is pretty cool too!

Formula

Fractals are interesting to look at, with their fascination mixture of complexity and structure. From sea-horses to lightning bolts, the emergent patterns often reflect those observed in nature.

All of the Mandelbrot and Julia fractal detail comes from the following iterative equation: zn+1 = zn 2 + c

Mandelbrot Set

All grid lines represent one unit. The x-axis is real-numbers, and the y-axis is imaginary numbers. The center of the graphs is the point (0 + 0·i), the top right is (2 + 2·i), and the bottom left is (-2 - 2·i). The graphs on the right show each step of the iterative calculation. The red circle has a radius of 2, any iterative step that exceeds this magnitude is guaranteed to diverge.

Mouse-over the Mandelbrot graph to reveal the iterative calcualtions for the point under your cursor. For each iteration a line is drawn on the smaller graphs to the right. When you mouse-over the black region the equation does not diverge, so the lines will always stay with the red boundaries.

Click on the Mandelbrot set to re-draw a new Julia Set for the corresponding constant "c" value. The Julia Set pattern will resemble the pattern in the small area around where you click.

About

From Wikipedia/Fractal:

A fractal has been defined as "a rough or fragmented geometric shape that can be split into parts, each of which is (at least approximately) a reduced-size copy of the whole," a property called self-similarity. Roots of the idea of fractals go back to the 17th century, while mathematically rigorous treatment of fractals can be traced back to functions studied by Karl Weierstrass, Georg Cantor and Felix Hausdorff a century later in studying functions that were continuous but not differentiable; however, the term fractal was coined by Benoît Mandelbrot in 1975 and was derived from the Latin frāctus meaning "broken" or "fractured." A mathematical fractal is based on an equation that undergoes iteration, a form of feedback based on recursion. There are several examples of fractals, which are defined as portraying exact self-similarity, quasi self-similarity, or statistical self-similarity. While fractals are a mathematical construct, they are found in nature, which has led to their inclusion in artwork. They are useful in medicine, soil mechanics, seismology, and technical analysis.
